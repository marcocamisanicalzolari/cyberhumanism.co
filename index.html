The Manifesto

Why Cyberhumanism

We live in a time of radical transformation. Artificial intelligence is reshaping how we work, decide, communicate, govern, and relate to each other. The speed of change has outpaced our capacity to understand its consequences.

We are not facing a simple technological upgrade. We are facing a redefinition of what it means to be human in a world where machines learn, generate, persuade, and make decisions that were once ours alone.

Previous philosophical frameworks addressed the digital revolution in broad terms. Digital Humanism, born in Vienna in 2019, called for technology aligned with human values. That call was necessary. But it was not enough.

The word "digital" describes a format. The word "cyber" describes a relationship: between humans, machines, networks, and the intelligence that connects them. The challenge we face today is not about digital tools. It is about systems that think, act, and influence, often without our knowledge, sometimes without our consent.

Cyberhumanism is a response to this specific moment. It is a philosophy born from direct experience inside the institutions that govern AI, from years of translating complex technology for millions of people, and from the conviction that humans must remain the authors of their own decisions.

The Principles

What We Believe

I
Humans decide. Machines assist.

No algorithm should make a consequential decision about a human life without meaningful human oversight. The augmented human, empowered by intelligent tools, is the future. The replaced human is not.

II
Ethics begins at design, not after deployment.

Embedding ethical considerations into AI systems from the first line of code is not an obstacle to innovation. It is a condition for trustworthy innovation. Responsible design is not a constraint. It is a competitive advantage.

III
Transparency is non-negotiable.

Citizens have the right to know when they are interacting with AI, how their data is being used, and why a system made a specific decision. Opacity in AI systems is a threat to democracy.

IV
AI governance requires insiders, not only observers.

Philosophy and policy must be informed by people who have worked inside the systems they seek to govern. Theory without institutional practice produces rules that no one can implement and no one will follow.

V
Digital literacy is a fundamental right.

Every citizen must have the knowledge to understand, question, and navigate AI-mediated environments. Digital illiteracy in the age of artificial intelligence is a form of disenfranchisement.

VI
Democracy cannot be delegated to algorithms.

Electoral processes, public discourse, and institutional decision-making must remain under human control. When AI shapes political opinion at scale, it is not serving democracy. It is undermining it.

VII
The vulnerable must be protected first.

Children, minorities, elderly populations, and communities with limited digital access bear the highest risk from unregulated AI. Protection of the most vulnerable is not an afterthought. It is the first test of any AI system.

VIII
AI is global. Governance must be too.

No single nation or bloc can regulate AI alone. Europe, the United States, and the rest of the world must find common ground on AI governance. Competition between regulatory models should produce better standards, not regulatory vacuums.

IX
Communication is a human act.

When AI generates content that influences, persuades, or informs, the responsibility remains with humans. Strategic communication in the age of AI requires understanding algorithms not as tools but as actors with their own logic and biases.

X
The future is not inevitable. It is a choice.

Transhumanism promises a future where machines transcend human limitations. Cyberhumanism proposes a future where humans use machines to amplify their own capabilities, judgment, and creativity. The plumber of the future is not a robot. It is a human with superpowers granted by technology.

"We must dominate artificial intelligence, not be dominated by it. The augmented human is the path forward: intelligent technology as an instrument that amplifies human capabilities."
Marco Camisani Calzolari
The Author

Marco Camisani Calzolari

Marco Camisani Calzolari coined the concept of Cyberhumanism and published the foundational book Cyberumanesimo (Il Sole 24 Ore, 2024). He is not a philosopher writing from outside the systems he critiques. He is a practitioner who has worked inside the institutions that govern artificial intelligence.

He was a member of the European Commission's General Purpose AI Code of Practice Working Group, shaping the rules that govern AI systems across Europe. He serves on Italy's National AI Strategy Committee at the Presidency of the Council of Ministers and is an expert by decree at Italy's National Cybersecurity Agency.

He has addressed the United Nations in New York on AI risks for minorities, the UK Parliament's House of Lords on AI and democracy, and the Italian Chamber of Deputies with a Lectio Magistralis on the future of intelligent systems.

An Adjunct Professor of Cyber-Humanities at Università Vita-Salute San Raffaele in Milan, he is also a science communicator whose work reaches approximately 25 million monthly video views across digital platforms. He is a Knight of the Order of the Star of Italy and a Freeman of the City of London.

He lives in New York City. camisanicalzolari.com
